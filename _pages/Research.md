## job market paper
**Electoral Turnover and Government Efficiency: Evidence from Federal Procurement**   

<p style="text-align: justify;"><font size="3">
Do political actors attempt to "lock in" their preferred policies in response to anticipated electoral turnover, and does this generate government inefficiencies? Conventional accounts suggest that executive agencies expecting waning power due to electoral turnover have incentives to insulate their preferred policies through cumbersome and inefficient bureaucratic procedures. This argument is premised on an assumption that adversaries will find it difficult or impossible to overturn those policies via formal means. But in numerous policy areas, those adversaries may have informal means to do so at their disposal that are far easier to implement. In those areas, agencies facing the threat of electoral turnover may find it in their interest to reflect the future Congress's preference so that their policies survive after the congressional turnover. To the extent that those future Congresses care about government performance, this shift in behavior can enhance efficiency, especially if executive branch officials would have preferred inefficient particularistic policies in the absence of any threat to their electoral dominance.</font></p>   

<p style="text-align: justify;"><font size="3">
 I evaluate these incentives in the context of the federal procurement, where federal agencies under unified government may prefer giving non-competitive, higher-cost contracts to firms that donated to the president's party in the absence of any threat to their political dominance. Using data on over 10 million federal procurement contracts in the United States, I find that as the probability of future congressional turnover increases, federal agencies under unified government are more likely to provide lower-cost contracts through competitive bidding, in the expectation that the opposition Congress might overturn non-competitive contracts given to the president's donors. My findings challenge the dominant perspective that electoral turnover degrades bureaucratic performance.</font></p>
