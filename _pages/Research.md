## job market paper
Electoral Turnover and Government Efficiency: Evidence from Federal Procurement   

<p style="text-align: justify;"><font size="3">
Abstract: Do political actors attempt to "lock in" their preferred policies in response to anticipated electoral turnover, and does this generate government inefficiencies? Conventional accounts suggest that public officials facing electoral loss have incentives to insulate their preferred policies through cumbersome and inefficient bureaucratic procedures. This argument is premised on an assumption that adversaries will find it difficult or impossible to overturn those policies via formal means.  But in numerous policy areas, those adversaries may have informal means at their disposal that are far easier to implement. In those areas, officials facing the threat of an electoral loss may find it in their interest to pursue efficient policy today as a way to avoid reversals tomorrow. I test this hypothesis by examining the effect of unanticipated shocks to the likelihood of congressional turnover on the probability of executive agencies choosing competitive bidding, conditional on procurement contracts being provided. Using data on over 10 million federal procurement contracts in the United States, I find that as the probability of future congressional turnover increases, federal agencies under the unified government becomes less inclined to choose the non-competitive procedure in industries where many firms are connected to the president, thereby engaging more in competitive bidding. My findings challenge the dominant perspective that electoral turnover degrades bureaucratic performance.</font></p>
